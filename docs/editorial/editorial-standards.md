# Editorial Standards

This document is the operational companion to the Editorial section of the Operating System. The OS contains the principles; this document contains the rules that implement those principles.

Every person who writes, edits, or publishes content on ToolKno — including the founder, any future contributors, and any AI-assisted content workflows — must follow these standards without exception.

---

## 1. Minimum Testing Requirements

| Content type | Minimum testing period | Testing definition |
|---|---|---|
| Review | 21 days | Active daily or near-daily use for the software's primary purpose |
| Comparison | 21 days per product compared | Each product in the comparison must meet the minimum individually |
| Buying guide | N/A (references existing reviews) | Must link to published ToolKno reviews; no new product evaluations within the guide itself |
| Tool page | 7 days | Using the ToolKno tool category to evaluate claims; not a vendor product review |

"Active use" means using the product to accomplish real work, not exploring the interface. A 21-day test of a grammar checker means using it on real writing for 21 days. A 21-day test of a newsletter platform means planning, writing, and sending at least two newsletters using the platform.

---

## 2. Testing Documentation Requirements

Before beginning a review, create a testing log. The log must contain:

1. **Product name and version/date** — products change; document which version was tested
2. **Testing goals** — what specific capabilities are being evaluated
3. **Testing period start and end dates**
4. **Daily or near-daily notes** — observations as they happen, not reconstructed after testing ends
5. **Configuration notes** — what account tier was used, what integrations were tested, what was not tested and why
6. **Week 1 impressions vs. Week 3 impressions** — note any changes in evaluation over time

The testing log is retained internally. It does not need to be published, but it must exist and be referenced during drafting.

---

## 3. Evidence Requirements

Every claim in published editorial content requires evidence of exactly one of these types:

**Type 1: Direct observation.** Written in first person. Example: "During three weeks of testing, the Grammarly autocorrect overrode my deliberate stylistic choices four times without warning."

**Type 2: Verified vendor documentation.** Linked to a primary source. Example: "Grammarly's Business plan costs $15 per member per month (source: grammarly.com/plans, verified June 2026)."

**Type 3: Named third-party data.** From a primary source, not a secondary source citing the primary. Example: "Grammarly has over 30 million daily active users (source: Grammarly press kit, 2025)."

**Type 4: Direct user interview.** Spoke with at least three independent, identified users. Quotes or paraphrases their specific experience. Example: "Three academic writers I interviewed described the citation suggestion feature as inconsistent — reliable on formal documents but frequently wrong on informal academic writing."

**Not permitted as evidence:** "Users generally find...", "Most experts agree...", "Studies show...", unattributed statistics, social media impressions, claims that cannot be verified.

## 4. Review Structure

Every review follows this structure. Sections may be renamed for specific products but must contain this content.

**1. Verdict (above the fold)**
One sentence stating the recommendation and for whom. Example: "Grammarly is the right choice for writers who need consistent grammar and spelling correction and value the AI rewrite suggestions — but not for writers who prize their stylistic voice above all else."

**2. Who this review is for**
One paragraph defining the reader this review will most help. This prevents the review from trying to serve all possible readers equally.

**3. What I tested and how**
Two to three paragraphs describing the testing period, testing conditions, account tier, and what was and was not tested.

**4. What works**
The genuine strengths. Specific. Evidence-backed. Not a list of marketing claims.

**5. What doesn't work**
The genuine weaknesses. This section must exist in every review. A review with no weaknesses section is not credible.

**6. Pricing**
Current pricing as of the review date. Always includes: free tier details (if any), paid tier starting price, what the paid tier adds, and the plan the reviewer tested on.

**7. Alternatives to consider**
At minimum two alternatives, with one sentence explaining when each is the better choice. Links to ToolKno comparisons and reviews when they exist.

**8. The verdict, expanded**
Two to three paragraphs expanding on the opening verdict with nuance. Who should not buy this product. What would need to change in the product for the verdict to change.

**9. Update history**
A table of any changes made to the review after publication, with dates and reasons.

---

## 5. Comparison Structure

**1. Use case definition**
Exactly one sentence defining the specific decision this comparison helps. Example: "This comparison helps you choose between Grammarly and ProWritingAid if you are a full-time writer or editor who uses a grammar checker daily."

**2. Summary verdict**
Which product wins for the defined use case, and why in one sentence.

**3. Criteria**
A table of 5–8 criteria, each evaluated for both products with a brief explanation. Include: accuracy, speed/performance, usability, pricing, key features, what's missing.

**4. Head-to-head on each criterion**
One section per criterion (≥ 2 paragraphs each). Based on testing evidence, not specs.

**5. Who should choose Product A**
Specific. "If you [specific condition], choose Grammarly."

**6. Who should choose Product B**
Specific. "If you [specific condition], choose ProWritingAid."

**7. Affiliate disclosure**
Inline per Constitution Rule 2.

---

## 6. Buying Guide Structure

**1. What this guide covers**
The specific purchase decision and the reader it serves.

**2. The criteria that matter (for this use case)**
This is editorial. Not every criterion matters for every use case. The guide defines which criteria are decision-relevant for the specific reader.

**3. Top recommendations with rationale**
Each recommendation links to the full ToolKno review. Does not repeat the full review — references it.

**4. What to avoid (and why)**
At least one product that people commonly consider but that the guide recommends against for the specific use case, with reasons.

**5. Pricing comparison**
A table comparing current pricing for the top 3–5 recommendations.

## 7. Affiliate Disclosure Standard

**At article level** (appears at top of any article with affiliate links):
> "ToolKno earns a commission if you purchase products through some links in this article. This does not affect which products we recommend or how we evaluate them. See our [Editorial Independence Policy]."

**At link level** (appears adjacent to every individual affiliate link):
> (affiliate link — ToolKno earns a commission if you purchase through this link)

No article may contain affiliate links without both disclosure types. If either is missing, the article fails Constitution Rule 2.

## 8. Update Policy

| Trigger | Action | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| 12 months since publication | Full audit: re-test key features, update all factual claims, update pricing | Within 30 days of the 12-month anniversary |
| Major price change | Update pricing section | Within 7 days |
| Feature removed | Update features section, reconsider verdict | Within 7 days |
| Product acquired | Add acquisition notice, flag for full audit | Notice within 7 days, audit within 90 days |
| Verdict changes | Document change in Update History section | Same day as change |
| Affiliate program terms change | Update disclosure language if needed | Within 7 days |

## 9. Factual Accuracy Standards

All factual claims are verified at publication time against primary sources:

- **Pricing:** Verified from the product's official pricing page. Include verification date in the article.
- **Feature claims:** Verified by direct testing or official documentation, not from third-party reviews.
- **Company information:** Verified from official company sources (About page, press releases, LinkedIn).
- **Statistics:** From named primary sources with publication dates. If a statistic is older than 24 months, it is labelled as such or updated.

After publication, a factual error discovered by a reader or via self-audit is corrected within 24 hours and noted in the Update History section. The correction notice reads: "Correction, [date]: [original claim] was incorrect. [Correct claim]. Thanks to [source if applicable]."

---

## 10. Voice and Tone

**Voice:** First-person, specific, direct. Not hedged to the point of saying nothing. "This product is suitable for many types of users" is not a sentence that belongs in a ToolKno review.

**Tone:** Professional but not academic. Confident. The reader should feel that they are getting the opinion of someone who has genuinely used the product and has something specific to say.

**Avoid:**
- Excessive use of passive voice
- Marketing language adopted from the vendor's own copy
- Superlatives without evidence ("the most powerful," "the best")
- Hedged language that avoids commitment ("may be," "could potentially," "some users might find")
