# Revenue & Distribution Strategy

This document contains realistic revenue projections, the affiliate program directory, distribution platform rankings, a sustainable content schedule, and the 20 biggest affiliate business mistakes. All projections are brutally honest — not aspirational.

---

## Revenue Projections

### Assumptions

- One founder, 15–20 hours/week available for content
- Traffic from organic search (no paid acquisition)
- Newsletter list is being built from zero
- Affiliate conversion rate: 0.5–2% of targeted page sessions (not total traffic)
- Average affiliate commission per conversion: $20–$50 (writing tools) → $50–$150 (creator tools)

### Projection Table

| Month | Worst Case | Expected | Best Case | Primary Driver |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Month 1 | $3 | $50 | $211 | AdSense from existing tool traffic only |
| Month 3 | $18 | $150 | $680 | First affiliate commissions + newsletter beginning |
| Month 6 | $90 | $500 | $2,100 | 5+ reviews live, newsletter at 300+ subscribers |
| Month 12 | $399 | $1,200 | $5,400 | 15+ reviews, newsletter at 1,500+, first sponsorship possible |
| Month 24 | $1,200 | $3,800 | $12,000 | Creator niche established, newsletter at 5,000+, sponsorships active |

**Worst case assumptions:** One Google algorithm update suppresses all tool traffic. Newsletter confirmation rate stays below 30%. First 6 months produce no affiliate revenue because reviews are not ranking.

**Expected case assumptions:** Organic tool traffic holds. Reviews take 3–6 months to rank. Newsletter grows steadily. First affiliate commission in month 2–3.

**Best case assumptions:** One or two reviews rank in top 5 for high-volume keywords early. Newsletter conversion exceeds 1.5% of tool traffic. Creator tool reviews earn high-commission conversions.

### Revenue Breakdown by Source (Month 12, Expected Case)

| Source | Monthly Revenue | % of Total |
|---|---|---|
| Affiliate — Writing tools | $600 | 50% |
| Affiliate — Creator tools (early) | $300 | 25% |
| Google AdSense | $200 | 17% |
| Newsletter sponsorship (if list ≥ 1,500) | $100 | 8% |
| **Total** | **$1,200** | 100% |

## Content Schedule (One Founder, 15–20 hours/week)

### Weekly rhythm

| Day | Activity | Hours |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Testing session (active software testing, notes) | 2h |
| Tuesday | Writing session (review draft, comparison draft, or newsletter draft) | 3h |
| Wednesday | Product development (engineering, tool page work) | 4h |
| Thursday | Writing session continuation | 3h |
| Friday | Publishing, distribution, SEO tasks | 2h |
| Weekend | Testing continuation if needed | 2h (optional) |

**Total: 14–16 active hours/week. 15–20 hours/week total including context switching.**

### Monthly output targets

| Content type | Target/month | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Reviews | 1–2 | Each review requires 21+ days of testing; 1 per month is sustainable and high-quality |
| Comparisons | 1 | Derived from completed reviews; relatively fast to write |
| Buying guides | 0–1 | Quarterly cadence more realistic |
| Newsletter issues | 2 | Biweekly cadence — fixed commitment |
| Tool page updates | 2–4 | Existing pages, not new tools |

**Do not attempt more than this.** Quality controls volume. One excellent review ranks and converts. Four rushed reviews contribute to the long-term erosion of editorial credibility.

## 20 Biggest Affiliate Business Mistakes

Documented so they can be recognized before they happen.

1. **Reviewing products for their affiliate rate, not their relevance.** A product that pays 0% but is the right recommendation for your audience is more valuable long-term than a 40% commission on a product nobody should buy.

2. **Publishing reviews without using the product.** This is detectable by sophisticated readers. It destroys trust permanently with the specific readers who matter most — the ones who test recommendations.

3. **Burying the disclosure.** Footer-only disclosure, disclosure-free category pages, and boilerplate disclosure pages are not sufficient. Disclosure must be at the point of the affiliate link.

4. **Optimizing review headlines for SEO before optimizing for the reader.** "Best Grammarly Alternative for Writers in 2026 (We Tested All 7)" is better for both SEO and readers than "Top Grammarly Alternatives."

5. **Building a review site that only publishes positive reviews.** If every reviewed product gets 4–5 stars, your rating system is meaningless. Publish mediocre and negative verdicts.

6. **Not updating reviews when products change.** A favorable Grammarly review published in 2024 that no longer reflects the current product (pricing changed, feature removed) is misleading advertising, not editorial content.

7. **Accepting sponsored or vendor-provided content.** Even if labeled, it signals that your editorial is for sale.

8. **Relying on a single affiliate program for > 50% of revenue.** If that program terminates, changes terms, or the product goes out of business, the business is in crisis.

9. **Chasing high search volume keywords before establishing authority.** Targeting "best project management software" on Month 1 produces no results. Targeting "best grammar checkers for academic writers" on Month 1 can rank.

10. **Not building the email list from Day 1.** Every month without a newsletter is a month of acquired trust that cannot be re-captured.

11. **Publishing comparison articles where the "winner" always has the highest affiliate commission.** Sophisticated readers and search quality reviewers notice this pattern.

12. **Treating the free tools as a loss leader rather than a genuine product.** Users who sense that the tools exist only to funnel them into affiliate recommendations do not trust the recommendations.

13. **Publishing content in too many niches simultaneously.** Authority is niche-specific. A writing tool review from a site that also reviews enterprise CRM software carries less credibility than one from a dedicated writing tool authority.

14. **Not tracking which pages are driving affiliate conversions.** Without conversion attribution, it's impossible to know what content is worth producing more of.

15. **Ignoring list hygiene.** Sending to unengaged subscribers suppresses open rate, which suppresses deliverability, which means fewer people see the content. Quarterly list cleanup is not optional.

16. **Setting unrealistic expectations in newsletter welcome emails.** Promising weekly deep dives then delivering biweekly summaries destroys subscriber trust faster than any other single action.

17. **Applying for affiliate programs before the content exists.** Most programs require a content-bearing site for approval. Apply after the first review in a category is published.

18. **Not having an affiliate link registry.** Without a record of every affiliate link and the commission rate for every product reviewed, it's impossible to audit for editorial/revenue conflicts.

19. **Launching a newsletter before having a reason to send it.** The first three issues determine whether a subscriber stays or leaves. The first issue must contain original editorial content, not "coming soon" placeholders.

20. **Not reading the affiliate program terms before promoting.** Common violations: disclosing affiliate relationships incorrectly per FTC/platform guidelines, using brand names in ways prohibited by program terms, promoting on platforms not allowed by the program.
